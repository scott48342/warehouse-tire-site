# How to Read Tire Sizes: Complete Guide for Beginners

Ever looked at the side of your tire and seen something like **P275/55R20** and wondered what it means? You're not alone. Understanding tire sizes is essential for buying the right tires for your vehicle.

## Breaking Down the Tire Size Code

Let's use **P275/55R20** as our example:

### P = Tire Type
- **P** = Passenger vehicle tire
- **LT** = Light Truck tire (heavier duty)
- **ST** = Special Trailer tire
- No letter = European metric tire

### 275 = Width in Millimeters
This is the **width of the tire** from sidewall to sidewall when mounted. 275mm equals about 10.8 inches.

Common widths:
- **205-225mm** = Compact cars
- **235-265mm** = Sedans and crossovers
- **275-305mm** = Trucks and SUVs
- **315-355mm** = Performance cars and lifted trucks

### 55 = Aspect Ratio
This is the **sidewall height** as a percentage of the width. A 55 aspect ratio means the sidewall is 55% as tall as the tire is wide.

- **Lower number (30-45)** = Shorter sidewall, sportier look, stiffer ride
- **Higher number (55-75)** = Taller sidewall, more comfortable, better off-road

### R = Construction Type
- **R** = Radial (99% of modern tires)
- **B** = Bias-ply (rare, specialty use)
- **D** = Diagonal (very rare)

### 20 = Wheel Diameter in Inches
This tells you what size wheel the tire fits. A 20 means it fits a 20-inch wheel.

## Additional Tire Markings

### Load Index
Usually appears after the diameter (e.g., **P275/55R20 113**). The load index tells you how much weight each tire can support.

| Load Index | Max Load (lbs) |
|------------|----------------|
| 100 | 1,764 |
| 105 | 2,028 |
| 110 | 2,337 |
| 115 | 2,679 |
| 120 | 3,086 |

### Speed Rating
A letter after the load index (e.g., **P275/55R20 113H**). This indicates the maximum speed the tire is rated for.

| Rating | Max Speed |
|--------|-----------|
| S | 112 mph |
| T | 118 mph |
| H | 130 mph |
| V | 149 mph |
| W | 168 mph |
| Y | 186 mph |

## Common Tire Size Questions

### Can I Use a Different Tire Size?

**Yes, but be careful.** You can usually go slightly wider or taller, but:
- Stay within 3% of original diameter to avoid speedometer errors
- Check for fender clearance
- Maintain similar load capacity

### What's the Difference Between P and LT Tires?

**P-metric tires** are designed for passenger vehicles and prioritize comfort and fuel efficiency.

**LT tires** are built tougher for trucks and heavy loads. They have:
- Stronger sidewalls
- Higher load capacity
- Better puncture resistance
- Slightly rougher ride

### How Do I Find My Original Tire Size?

Check these locations:
1. **Driver's door jamb** - Look for a sticker with tire info
2. **Owner's manual** - Lists recommended sizes
3. **Current tires** - If they're original equipment
